Let's be honest: if you work in IT right now, your feed is basically 50% AI hype, 40% AI fear, and 10% confused memes about both. Depending on who you ask, AI is either coming for your job, coming for everyone's job, or going to "free you up to do more strategic work" (which somehow always looks a lot like "do the same work, just faster, with fewer people").

Some of the fear is legit. Companies are downsizing, slapping "AI-driven efficiency" on every layoff slide, and then turning around and handing the remaining team a to-do list that looks like a horror movie script. And some of it is pure misinformation or half-baked understanding of what these tools can actually do.

Either way, AI is going to enter your professional life. The choice is not "AI or no AI." It's "AI on your terms, or AI on someone else's." Let's talk about how to keep it on your terms.

## 1. Start With Privacy, Not Shiny Features

Before we talk about how AI can help your career, we need to talk about something very unsexy: privacy. Every new AI tool right now shows up promising to write your code, document your infrastructure, summarize your meetings and probably make your coffee if they can figure out the right integration. Behind the scenes, all of that hinges on where your data goes, how long it stays there, and who gets to see it.

Here are a few things that I like to keep in mind during my explorations into AI:

### A. Research the tools before you feed them your life

Especially installable tools. A desktop assistant that happily crawls your entire filesystem is very different from a constrained coding helper with a clearly defined scope.

For example:

- Tools such as Claude Code or Open AI Codex work out of a defined folder, and they stay politely in that sandbox until you explicitly point them elsewhere.
- Others like Claude Cowork or Microsoft Copilot are designed to act as your teammate and will leverage anything they've been given access to across documents, chats, and systems to help you.

Neither is inherently bad but you should know which one you're inviting into your environment before you hand it the keys.

### B. Stick (mostly) to the mainstream

Yes, the bleeding edge is fun. Yes, that GitHub repo with 3 stars and a README written at 3 a.m. might do something "mind-blowing" but it might also log your prompts in weird places, phone home to who-knows-where and treat data security as a "nice to have someday."

The bigger vendors aren't perfect, but they usually have:

- Legal teams who hate surprises
- Compliance checklists
- Actual documentation on retention and deletion

In a space changing weekly, letting someone else do at least some of the security heavy lifting is not the worst idea.

### C. Confirm there's a way to be forgotten

If you're shopping platforms (personally or for your team), don't just ask what can this do for me but also:

- "What happens when I leave?"
- "Can my data be fully deleted or disconnected?"
- "If this thing has been 'learning from my usage,' how much of me is baked into it?"

In the end make sure there's a real mechanism to be forgotten, and actually use it when you move on.

## 2. AI Isn't Coming for Your Job. The AI-Enabled Employee Is.

In most IT roles, no, you're probably not about to be neatly swapped out for a model and a bash script but you can absolutely be replaced by someone who knows your job, knows the tools and is getting the grunt work done 20-50% faster through use of AI tools.

Rather than leading with stubbornness it's worth thinking about these ways AI can positively effect you in your day to day:

- **Better workflows**: Using AI to rough out scripts, configs, test plans, or runbooks so you're editing instead of writing from scratch.
- **Extended capabilities**: Need to dip into a new technology? AI can help you ramp faster, translate docs, or walk you through examples instead of spending a week doom-scrolling Stack Overflow.
- **Automated documentation**: If a tool can turn your CLI history, code diff, or design notes into draft documentation, that's a win for everyone—especially Future You.
- **Proof-of-concept accelerator**: You can go from "I wonder if this would work…" to a rough PoC in an afternoon by pairing your expertise with an AI assistant.

Are these tools perfect? Absolutely not. That said if your default stance is "I don't trust it, I'm not touching it", versus "I don't trust it, but I'll make it work for me"… you can guess which one looks more "future ready" on paper.

In IT, refusing to adapt has never been a long-term career strategy. AI is just the latest version of that lesson.

## 3. AI Is Most Powerful in the Hands of a Subject Matter Expert

Here's the part that should actually make you feel better:

AI is at its best when it's paired with someone who already knows what they're doing.

We're still very much in the era of Human in the Loop (and frankly, we might stay here forever).

Anytime we're dealing with data or complex systems, we're still facing the same old problem: Garbage in, garbage out.

AI doesn't magically fix that. It just automates it at scale.

Without a real Subject Matter Expert (SME) in the loop, you get:

- **Unchecked hallucinations** – Confidently wrong answers, misleading "facts," broken code that almost works but fails in the one place that matters.
- **Inefficient designs and workflows** – Models picking suboptimal patterns, over-engineered solutions, or missing key constraints that a human SME would spot immediately.
- **Security issues** – Things that "pass tests" but violate policies, leak data, or expose attack surfaces no one asked for.

On the flip side, when you — the network engineer, sysadmin, SRE, architect, DBA, security analyst, etc. — are driving:

- You know when the output is nonsense.
- You see gaps in the logic, performance, or security posture.
- You understand which corners can be safely cut and which cannot.

You might not be a developer or a "content creator," but your domain knowledge is the multiplier. The model amplifies what you bring to the table; it doesn't replace it.

A mediocre SME + great AI = mediocre outcome.
A strong SME + decent AI = surprisingly great outcome.

## Conclusion: Make AI Work For Your Career, Not Against It

So where does all this leave you?

- Don't obsess over whether AI is coming for your job. Focus on how it can help you do your job better, faster, and with less drudgery.
- Be mindful of privacy. Every time you paste something into a prompt box, ask: "If this escaped into the wild, would I be okay with that?" Then choose tools and settings accordingly.
- Lead as the SME. The best results come when you decide what you're trying to do, how you want to do it, and then tell the AI exactly how to help.

AI is going to be part of your IT career one way or another. You can either be the person it "happens to" or the person who knows how to aim it.
