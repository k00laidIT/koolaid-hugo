This morning was the opening Keynote with Pat <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Gelsinger">Gelsinger</span> and the gang and while there were some announcements (more on that later) the key take away is that the time is upon us IT folk to be brave. It is a time to cast aside concerns about cloud and your job as it currently is done because your role will be automated and start giving all your money for <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="VMWare">VMWare</span>. No need to concern yourself with hardware, that can all be white boxed, but only think about things such as networking and storage as just small components of the greater Hyper-Converged compute nodes regardless of location. I tended to look upon this with great skepticism as I don't think we're quite ready or should be ready to put the network guys out to pasture, replaced with new shiny Virtualization <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Admins">Admins</span> who know what an IP address is and most of the time understand what a <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="VLAN">VLAN</span> does for you. ](large_image.jpg)But intermingled with the <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Mierda">Mierda</span> of Info Tech talk there were some new announcements, the biggest of which was that of a new product line, EVO, who purpose to be be the <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="hyperconverged">hyperconverged</span> boxes you are looking for, putting <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Vmware">Vmware</span> into direct competition with the likes of <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Nutanix">Nutanix</span> and what sounds like their own partnership VCE <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vBlock">vBlock</span>. In the case of the former the EVO:Rail is the ready for primetime product, with actual hardware available here to look and see through any of their partners on the product. The idea is that within a single 2U enclosure you have a series of mixed flash and spinning disks, 4 blades each containing 2 6 core CPUs, 192 GB of RAM, and 2 10 <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Gbps">Gbps</span> <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="uplinks">uplinks</span> augmented by a 1 <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Gbps">Gbps</span> link for <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="IPMI">IPMI</span> and remote management. All of this is powered by a <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vSphere">vSphere</span> Enterprise Plus level environment utilizing <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="VSAN">VSAN</span> and distributed <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vSwitch">vSwitch</span> masked out with a shiny new <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="HTML5">HTML5</span> based web client like interface. I have high hopes this means the end is near for the flash based <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vSphere">vSphere</span> Web Client. The second announcement of the EVO line is EVO:Rack which is coming soon, but it is essentially a roll in rack based solution with everything you need to start up or <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="exand">exand</span> your data center. If that sound familiar to you you've probably seen systems such as <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vBlock">vBlock</span> and <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="FlexPod">FlexPod</span>. In all the take away regarding the EVO product line is get your system up and running and start deploying VMs in an exceptionally small window of time. I will say was a bit disappointed when I actually met with the Dell folks regarding their version of the EVO:Rail product in that it provides an effective 13 TB of available <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="datastore">datastore</span> space and the system provides no ability to add external <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="datastores">datastores</span> at all. Back into <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Vmware's">Vmware's</span> bread and butter in the software space there was a good deal of announcement of new versions and rebranding of their product line including <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vSphere">vSphere</span> 6 being in beta which we already knew, the the <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vCloud">vCloud</span> Hybrid product being rebranded as <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vCloud">vCloud</span> Air, and <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vCloud">vCloud</span> Automation Center (<span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vCAC">vCAC</span>) was renamed <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="vRealize">vRealize</span>, both of which with a few <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="noticable">noticable</span> upgrades. In the training space we now have a <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Vmware">Vmware</span> certification track for us network folks centered around the NSX product. These certifications include the now available <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="VCP-NV">VCP-NV</span> and the soon to be released <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="VCIX">VCIX</span> (<span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Vmware">Vmware</span> Certified <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="Internetworking">Internetworking</span> Expert) and <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="VCDX-NV">VCDX-NV</span>. Interestingly they are providing horizontal opt outs for those of us already network certified by for a short period time allowing <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="CCNAs">CCNAs</span> and <span class="scayt-misspell-word" data-scayt-word="CCNPs">CCNPs</span> to take the exam without a course requirements. If you are a current VCP you will also be able to take the exam without course requirements.
